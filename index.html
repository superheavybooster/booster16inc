STARSHIP'S SECOND FLIGHT TEST

Starship Integrated Flight Test 2 is our second flight which will hopefully be successful. Check timeline for events.

Mission Overview

We are planning to have a soft splashdown in the Gulf Of Mexico for both the Super Heavy Booster and the Starship Upper Stage including an in-space relight of a raptor engine to demonstrate de-orbit burns. We are not doing any tricky test like disabling engines yet on these early flights. We wanna demonstrate how an operational launch would perform.

  • • Goal: Soft splashdown for both the Super Heavy Booster and the Starship Upper Stage.
  • • Payload: None.
  • • Target Orbit: Suborbital Trajectory.

Updates

First Flight. First Lessons.

Date: 26/9/2025

The Starship launch began nominally, with all 33 Raptor engines igniting successfully and the vehicle passing through Max-Q. Stage separation proceeded as planned, and the Starship upper stage lit all six of its engines without issue. Despite the challenges that followed, these moments demonstrated that critical systems such as engine ignition, stage separation, and initial upper stage operations were functioning as intended.

The Super Heavy Booster encountered multiple setbacks after Max-Q. Three engines failed shortly after liftoff, and two of these could not relight during the boostback burn. Additional engine failures occurred throughout the boostback, leaving only three operational engines by the time the vehicle began its coast to the landing burn. At approximately 5 km altitude, the booster was lost. Structural issues were also noted, with one grid fin ripped off during Max-Q and another lost during ascent.

The Starship upper stage initially performed well, lighting all six engines at stage separation. However, the flight ended prematurely when the vehicle exploded at 80 km altitude, traveling at roughly 11000 km/h. While the ship completed its stage separation and engine ignition successfully, it was unable to sustain flight beyond the initial ascent phase, marking a critical failure in upper stage performance.

Preliminary analysis suggests that the Super Heavy Booster failures were caused by a combination of engine anomalies and structural stress during ascent. The early engine shutdowns likely resulted from a fuel mixture imbalance under high dynamic pressure, compounded by the loss of two grid fins that affected vehicle stability and aerodynamic loads. To address these issues, the engineering team plans to reinforce grid fin mounts, adjust Raptor fuel delivery algorithms, and implement additional sensors to monitor engine performance in real time. These upgrades aim to ensure that the booster can survive Max-Q and maintain thrust throughout boostback and landing sequences.

The Starship upper stage failure appears to have been triggered by an overpressure event in one of the main propellant tanks, likely exacerbated by thermal stress and the extreme velocity of 11000km/h. This led to structural compromise and the subsequent explosion at 80km altitude. To prevent a repeat, the team plans to reinforce the tank supports and upgrade the venting system to safely release excess pressure during high-speed ascent. Additional work will focus on strengthening the vehicle's aft section and optimizing engine startup sequences to reduce shock loads on the structure. These improvements aim to allow Starship to safely complete stage separation and sustain flight beyond the current altitude limits.

Next Flight. Next Lessons.

Date: 29/9/2025

The ascent burn of Flight 2 was flawless. The booster and ship cleared the pad cleanly, accelerated smoothly through max-Q, and all engines performed nominally through the early climb to orbit.

Following stage separation, the booster continued its mission with equal precision. Its boostback, controlled descent, and splashdown were executed without issue, marking our first ever splashdown recovery attempt and validating our landing profile.

The Starship upper stage ascended well at first, but fires began growing in the aft section before any engine issues. The first Raptor vacuum engine started failing at 15,800 km/h and fully failed shortly after. The second vacuum engine failed at 19,500 km/h, followed by the third at 20,600 km/h. The three sea-level engines failed almost simultaneously at 24,000 km/h, leaving the Starship with insufficient thrust and triggering the flight termination system.

The cause of the ship's failure has been conclusively identified as a fault in the redesigned aft section downcomer system. The new configuration introduced unanticipated flow instabilities at high mass-flow rates, resulting in intermittent propellant starvation, engine underperformance, and uncontrolled fires. To resolve this, we are reverting to the proven legacy downcomer design while continuing development of the new version with additional baffling and reinforcement. We are also implementing redundant flow sensors, enhanced fire shielding, and improved aft-section insulation to ensure reliable propellant delivery and containment on future flights.

Looking forward, the booster’s flawless performance and successful splashdown provide a strong foundation for future recoveries. While this flight was not a catch attempt, the data gathered will be used to refine our procedures and hardware in preparation for the first booster catch in upcoming missions.
